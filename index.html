How to Format Resumes for Recruiters

Based on recruiter insights — what actually gets your application read, not skipped.

BEFORE YOU SEND CHECKLIST
Subject & Opening:
• Subject line matches the tone of the role (bold for sales/marketing, precise for finance/ops)
• Opening line earns a read — no "I'm writing to express my interest"
• Removed every bracket: [Your Name], X years, [Tech Stack], etc.
Body:
• States a real pain point the company likely has — not a generic one
• Includes at least one verified number or result (no invented metrics)
• Mentions the CV/portfolio attachment explicitly
• Reads like you, not like a copy-pasted template
Attachments:
• CV is actually attached (check before hitting send)
• Portfolio link works and is set to public/viewable
• File names are professional (not "CV_final_final2.pdf")
Final Pass:
• Read it out loud once — if it sounds stiff, cut a sentence
• Confirm the company name and role title are correct
• Sent from a professional email address
WHAT NOT TO DO
Don't open with filler.
✗ "I'm writing to express my interest in the [Role] position at [Company]."
✓ Lead with the pain point or the result — the role and company come naturally after.
Don't list duties without outcomes.
✗ "I managed social media accounts and created content."
✓ "I grew a 12K-follower account to 40K and turned that into 200+ monthly enquiries."
Don't oversell with no proof.
✗ "I'm a hardworking, detail-oriented team player."
✓ Let one specific result imply the trait — don't name it.
Don't send the same template to every role.
✗ Identical body copy for a Graphic Designer and Data Analyst role with just the title swapped.
✓ Swap the pain point and result to match what that specific role actually solves.
Don't fake numbers.
✗ Rounding up or inventing metrics you can't speak to in an interview.
✓ If you don't have a number, describe the outcome plainly instead of guessing one.
Don't ignore the follow-up.
✗ Sending once and assuming silence means rejection.
✓ One polite follow-up after 5–7 business days is expected, not pushy.
THE FORMULA THAT WORKS
Your email body needs:
1. A beginning line that gets them reading — no "I'm writing to express"
2. Your responsibilities and results — kept short, with numbers
3. A pain point — every company has one. Find it, talk about it, position yourself as the person solving it.
4. List a result around that pain point if you can
SUBJECT LINE EXAMPLES BY ROLE
Social Media Manager:
• "Stop Searching — Your Social Media Manager Just Applied"
• "One Less Role to Fill"
• "Before You Review Another Social Profile…"
UI/UX Designer:
• "Your Users Have Opinions About Your Products"
• "Built for Better User Experiences"
• "Your Product Needs Better Experience Design"
Data Analyst:
• "Your Data Is Already Telling You What to Do"
• "Interpreting Data Beyond the Numbers"
• "Where Data Becomes Direction"
Virtual Assistant:
• "Consider This One Less Thing on Your To-Do List"
• "Because There Are Only 24 Hours in a Day"
• "You Shouldn't Be Doing Everything Yourself"
💡 Pro tip from a recruiter: "90% of the time, your email application matters. I've seen candidates get responses within minutes by writing: 'You haven't heard the last of me.' Sometimes it matters."
